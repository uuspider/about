<h3>3.12. Punctuation</h3>
<div class="syms">
.        _   ;        _  ?        _  \dotsm
/        _   \colon   _  \dotsb   _  \dotso
|        _   :        _  \dotsc   _  \ddots
,        _   !        _  \dotsi   _  \vdots
</div>
<p class="note">Note 1. The <tex>:</tex> by itself produces a colon with class-3 
(relation) spacing. The command <tex>\colon</tex> produces special spacing for 
use in constructions such as <tex>f\colon A\to B </tex> \(f\colon A\to B\).</p>
<p class="note">Note 2. Although the commands <tex>\cdots</tex> and 
<tex>\ldots</tex> are frequently used, we recommend the more semantically oriented 
commands <tex>\dotsb</tex> <tex>\dotsc</tex> <tex>\dotsi</tex> <tex>\dotsm</tex>
<tex>\dotso</tex> for most purposes (see 4.6).</p>

<h3>3.17. Named operators</h3>
<p>These operators are represented by a multiletter abbreviation.</p>
<div class="syms">
\arccos     \det        \liminf     \sinh
\arcsin     \dim        \limsup     \sup
\arctan     \exp        \ln         \tan
\arg        \gcd        \log        \tanh
\cos        \hom        \max        \varinjlim
\cosh       \inf        \min        \varprojlim
\cot        \injlim     \Pr         \varliminf
\coth       \ker        \projlim    \varlimsup
\csc        \lg         \sec
\deg        \lim        \sin
</div>
<p>To define additional named operators outside the above list, use the 
<tex>\DeclareMathOperator</tex> command; for example, after</p>
<pre><code>\DeclareMathOperator{\rank}{rank}
\DeclareMathOperator{\esssup}{ess\,sup}</code></pre>
<p>one could write</p>
<div class="syms">
\rank(x)
\esssup(y,z)
</div>
<p>The star form <tex>\DeclareMathOperator*</tex> creates an operator that 
takes limits in a displayed formula like <tex>sup</tex> or <tex>max</tex>.</p>
<p>When predefining such a named operator is problematic (e.g., when using one 
in the title or abstract of an article), there is an alternative form that can 
be used directly:</p>
<div class="syms">
\operatorname{rank}(x)
</div>

<p><tex>\xleftarrow</tex> and <tex>\xrightarrow</tex> produce arrows that extend

<h3>5.3. The \genfrac command</h3>
<p>The capabilities of <tex>\frac</tex>, <tex>\binom</tex>, and their variants

arguments. The last two correspond to <tex>\frac</tex>’s numerator and denominator; 
the ﬁrst two are optional delimiters (as seen in <tex>\binom</tex>); the third

<tex>\textstyle</tex>, <tex>\scriptstyle</tex>, and <tex>\scriptscriptstyle</tex>. 
If the third argument is left empty, the line thickness defaults to ‘normal’.</p>

<h2 id="delimiter">分隔符</h2>

<h3>6.1. Delimiter sizes</h3>
<p>Unless you indicate otherwise, delimiters in math formulas will remain at the 
standard size regardless of the height of the enclosed material. To get larger
sizes, you can either select a particular size using a <tex>\big...</tex> preﬁx 
(see below), or you can use <tex>\left</tex> and <tex>\right</tex> preﬁxes for 
autosizing.</p>
<p>The automatic delimiter sizing done by <tex>\left</tex> and <tex>\right</tex> 
has two limitations: First, it is applied mechanically to produce delimiters 
large enough to encompass the largest contained item, and second, the range of 
sizes has fairly large quantum jumps. This means that an expression that is 
inﬁnitesimally too large for a given delimiter size will get the next larger 
size, a jump of 6pt or so (3pt top and bottom) in normal-sized text. There are
two or three situations where the delimiter size is commonly adjusted. These 
adjustments are done using the following commands:</p>
<table class="centre display">
    <tr>
        <th>Delimiter<br>size</th>  <td>text<br>size</td>  
        <td><tex>\left<br>\right</tex></td>  
        <td><tex>\bigl<br>\bigr</tex></td>
        <td><tex>\Bigl<br>\Bigr</tex></td>  
        <td><tex>\biggl<br>\biggr</tex></td>  
        <td><tex>\Biggl<br>\Biggr</tex></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
        <th>Result</th>  <td>\((b)(\dfrac{c}{d})\)</td>   
        <td>\(\left(b\right)\left(\dfrac{c}{d}\right)\)</td> 
        <td>\(\bigl(b\bigr)\bigl(\dfrac{c}{d}\bigr)\)</td>   
        <td>\(\Bigl(b\Bigr)\Bigl(\dfrac{c}{d}\Bigr)\)</td>   
        <td>\(\biggl(b\biggr)\biggl(\dfrac{c}{d}\biggr)\)</td> 
        <td>\(\Biggl(b\Biggr)\Biggl(\dfrac{c}{d}\Biggr)\)</td> 
    </tr>
</table>
<p>The ﬁrst kind of adjustment is done for cumulative operators with limits, 
such as summation signs. With <tex>\left</tex> and <tex>\right</tex> the 
delimiters usually turn out larger than necessary, and using the <tex>\Big</tex> 
or <tex>\bigg</tex> sizes instead gives better results:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\left[\sum_i a_i\left\lvert\sum_j x_{ij}\right\rvert^p\right]^{1/p}</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\left[\sum_i a_i\left\lvert\sum_j x_{ij}\right\rvert^p\right]^{1/p}\]</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\biggl[\sum_i a_i\Bigl\lvert\sum_j x_{ij}\Bigr\rvert^p\biggr]^{1/p}</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\biggl[\sum_i a_i\Bigl\lvert\sum_j x_{ij}\Bigr\rvert^p\biggr]^{1/p}\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>The second kind of situation is clustered pairs of delimiters where 
<tex>\left</tex> and <tex>\right</tex> make them all the same size (because that 
is adequate to cover the encompassed material) but what you really want is to 
make some of the delimiters slightly larger to make the nesting easier to see.</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\left((a_1 b_1) - (a_2 b_2)\right)
\left((a_2 b_1) + (a_1 b_2)\right)</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\left((a_1 b_1) - (a_2 b_2)\right)
\left((a_2 b_1) + (a_1 b_2)\right)\]</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\bigl((a_1 b_1) - (a_2 b_2)\bigr)
\bigl((a_2 b_1) + (a_1 b_2)\bigr)</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\bigl((a_1 b_1) - (a_2 b_2)\bigr)
\bigl((a_2 b_1) + (a_1 b_2)\bigr)\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>The third kind of situation is a slightly oversize object in running text, 
such as \(\left\lvert\frac{b'}{d'}\right\rvert\) where the delimiters produced 
by <tex>\left</tex> and <tex>\right</tex> cause too much line spreading. In that 
case <tex>\bigl</tex> and <tex>\bigr</tex> can be used to produce delimiters 
that are larger than the base size but still able to ﬁt within the normal line 
spacing: \(\bigl\lvert\frac{b'}{d'}\bigr\rvert\).</p>

<h3>6.2. Vertical bar notations</h3>
<p>The use of the <code>|</code> character to produce paired delimiters is not 
recommended. There is an ambiguity about the directionality of the symbol that 
will in rare cases produce incorrect spacing—e.g., <tex>|k|=|-k|</tex> produces 
\(|k|=|−k|\). Using <tex>\lvert</tex> for a “left vert bar” and <tex>\rvert</tex> 
for a “right vert bar” whenever they are used in pairs will prevent this problem: 
compare \(\lvert -k\rvert\), produced by <tex>\lvert -k\rvert</tex>. For double 
bars there are analogous <tex>\lVert</tex> and <tex>\rVert</tex> commands. 
Recommended practice is to deﬁne suitable commands in the document preamble for 
any paired-delimiter use of vert bar symbols:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\newcommand{\abs}[1]{\lvert#1\rvert}
\newcommand{\norm}[1]{\lVert#1\rVert}</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\newcommand{\abs}[1]{\lvert#1\rvert}
\newcommand{\norm}[1]{\lVert#1\rVert}\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>whereupon <tex>\abs{z}</tex> would produce \(\abs{z}\) and 
<tex>\norm{v}</tex> would produce \(\norm{v}\).</p>

<h2 id="integralsandsums">积分和求和</h2>
<h3>8.1. Altering the placement of limits</h3>
<p>The limits on integrals, sums, and similar symbols are placed either to the 
side of or above and below the base symbol, depending on convention and context. 
MathJax has rules for automatically choosing one or the other, and most of the 
time the results are satisfactory. In the event they are not, there are three 
commands that can be used to inﬂuence the placement of the limits: 
<tex>\limits</tex>, <tex>\nolimits</tex>, <tex>\displaylimits</tex>. Compare</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\int_{\abs{x-x_z(t)}\lt X_0}
x^6(t)\phi(x)</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\newcommand{\abs}[1]{\lvert#1\rvert}
            \int_{\abs{x-x_z(t)}\lt X_0}x^6(t)\phi(x)\]</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\int\limits_{\abs{x-x_z(t)}\lt X_0}
x^6(t)\phi(x)</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\int\limits_{\abs{x-x_z(t)}\lt X_0}x^6(t)\phi(x)\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>The <tex>\limits</tex> command should follow immediately after the base symbol 
to which it applies, and its meaning is: shift the following subscript and/or 
superscript to the limits position, regardless of the usual convention for this 
symbol. <tex>\nolimits</tex> means to shift them to the side instead, and 
<tex>\displaylimits</tex>, which might be used in deﬁning a new kind of base
symbol, means to use standard positioning as for the <tex>\sum</tex> command.</p>

    </tr>
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\iiiint\limits_A f(w,x,y,z)\;dw\;dx\;dy\;dz</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\iiiint\limits_A f(w,x,y,z)\;dw\;dx\;dy\;dz\]</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\idotsint\limits_A f(x_1,\dotsc,x_k)</code></pre></td>

    </tr>

<p>There’s also a command called <tex>\sideset</tex>, for a rather
special purpose: putting symbols at the subscript and superscript corners of a 
symbol like \(\sum\) or \(\prod\). <i>Note: The <tex>\sideset</tex> command is 
only designed for use with large operator symbols; with ordinary symbols the 
results are unreliable.</i> With <tex>\sideset</tex>, you can write</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\sideset{}{’}
  \sum_{n\lt k,\;\text{$n$ odd}} nE_n</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\sideset{}{’}
\sum_{n\lt k,\;\text{$n$ odd}} nE_n\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>The extra pair of empty braces is explained by the fact that <tex>\sideset</tex> 
has the capability of putting an extra symbol or symbols at each corner of a large 
operator; to put an asterisk at each corner of a product symbol, you would type</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\sideset{_*^*}{_*^*}\prod</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\sideset{_*^*}{_*^*}\prod\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
***
<h2 id="size">局部字号调整</h2>
<p>公式中的个别字符大小可以调整，如： <tex>\large</tex> ， <tex>\huge</tex>:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>{\large \#}\;{\Large \#}\;{\LARGE \#}
  \quad{\huge \#}\;{\Huge \#}</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[{\large \#}\;{\Large \#}\;{\LARGE \#}\quad{\huge \#}\;{\Huge \#}\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>If you want a \(\#\) symbol analogous to a summation sign in its typographical 
properties, then the best way to achieve that is to deﬁne it as a symbol of type 
“mathop” with the <tex>\DeclareMathSymbol</tex> command.</p>
<p>Consider the expression:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\frac{\sum_{n > 0} z^n}
  {\prod_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)}</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\frac{\sum_{n > 0} z^n}
{\prod_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)}\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>Using <tex>\dfrac</tex> instead of <tex>\frac</tex> wouldn’t change anything 
in this case; if you want the sum and product symbols to appear full size, you 
need the <tex>\displaystyle</tex> command:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\frac{ {\displaystyle\sum_{n > 0} z^n} }
  { {\displaystyle\prod_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)} }</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\frac{ {\displaystyle\sum_{n > 0} z^n} }
{ {\displaystyle\prod_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)} }\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>And if you want full-size symbols but with limits on the side, use the 
<tex>\nolimits</tex> command also:</p>
<table style="width: 100%;" class="centre">
    <tr>
        <td class="src"><pre><code>\frac{ {\displaystyle\sum\nolimits_{n> 0} z^n} }
  { {\displaystyle\prod\nolimits_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)} }</code></pre></td>
        <td class="eqn">\[\frac{ {\displaystyle\sum\nolimits_{n> 0} z^n} }
{ {\displaystyle\prod\nolimits_{1\leq k\leq n} (1-q^k)} }\]</td>
    </tr>
</table>
<p>There are similar commands <tex>\textstyle</tex>, <tex>\scriptstyle</tex>, 
and <tex>\scriptscriptstyle</tex>, to force MathJax to use the symbol size and 
spacing that would be applied in (respectively) inline math, ﬁrst-order 
subscript, or second-order order subscript, even when the current context would 
normally yield some other size.</p>
